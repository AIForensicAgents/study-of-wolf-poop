# study-of-wolf-poop 🐺💩

A focused, educational repository documenting wolf scat (scat, droppings, feces) for biological field-study and outreach purposes. This project aggregates observations, morphology notes, and habitat clues to help students, naturalists, and researchers better interpret field evidence while prioritizing safety and ethics.

---

## Overview

This repository is intended as a curated, responsibly-presented compendium for:
- Recognizing and describing wolf scat in the field
- Inferring dietary and ecological information from scat morphology and contents
- Supporting non-invasive monitoring and natural history education

Content is descriptive and observational; it is not a how-to for laboratory procedures or wildlife handling. The goal is to make field sign interpretation more accessible and scientifically grounded while keeping a light, engaging tone.

---

## Why it matters

- Scat is a low-impact source of ecological information. It can reveal diet composition, seasonal foraging shifts, and habitat use without capturing or stressing animals.
- Consistent scat observations can contribute to habitat management, conservation planning, and community science projects.
- Teaching people to interpret scat correctly reduces misidentification of predator presence and helps avoid unnecessary conflict or fear.

---

## Morphology notes

Observational criteria to record and compare:
- **Size & shape:** Wolf scat is typically tubular to tapered; diameter often correlates with animal size and recent food intake.
- **Length & segmentation:** Scat may appear segmented or continuous depending on diet and time since deposition.
- **Consistency & texture:** Ranges from firm to soft — high bone content or fur produces firmer scats; berry- or plant-rich diets produce softer, sometimes pulpy scats.
- **Color & sheen:** Fresh scat often appears dark and glossy; drying and exposure bleach color and change texture.
- **Contents (visible):** Hair, bone fragments, hooves, feather quills, fur tufts, insect parts, seeds, and berry skins are commonly visible; these give strong diet clues.
- **Odor:** A strong, musky scent may indicate freshness — note odor descriptively but avoid close contact.

Recording consistent morphological descriptors (e.g., diameter in cm, approximate length, moisture level, visible contents) increases dataset usefulness.

## Habitat / diet clues

Use scat in context with other field signs:
- **Location context:** Scat on game trails, near kill sites, or at latrines conveys different behavioral information (travel route vs. territorial marking vs. feeding).
- **Associated tracks:** Paw prints, stride length, and track pattern can help confirm species and group size.
- **Prey evidence:** Presence of bone, fur, hoof fragments, or feathers helps identify prey species and predation vs. scavenging.
- **Seasonal indicators:** Berry seeds, plant matter, and insect parts can indicate seasonal dietary shifts (e.g., summer berries vs. winter ungulate consumption).
- **Density & distribution:** Scat clustering and placement (e.g., elevated logs, trail intersections) can relate to territoriality and communication.

When possible, pair scat records with geotagged photos, date, vegetation type, and weather to enhance ecological interpretation.

## Ethics & safety

Safety and ethics are paramount when working with wildlife sign.

Field safety — general guidance:
- Observe from a distance. Do not approach animals or disturb their behavior.
- Wear disposable gloves and use tools (e.g., stick, trowel) if moving or sampling objects for photography or measurement.
- Avoid ingesting, inhaling, or getting scat in eyes or open wounds.
- Carry hand sanitizer and clean hands thoroughly after fieldwork; wash clothing and equipment if heavily soiled.
- Follow local regulations regarding handling of wildlife remains and biological materials.

Ethics & legal considerations:
- Do not harass or intentionally disturb animals to obtain scat.
- Obtain permission for access to private or protected lands.
- Respect indigenous and local community protocols regarding wildlife and field research.
- If using scat data for publication, follow appropriate data-sharing and attribution practices. Sensitive location data (e.g., den sites, rare species locations) may require redaction to prevent harm.

Note: This repository intentionally avoids procedural lab protocols (e.g., DNA extraction or pathogen culturing). For genetic or pathogen work, consult institutional labs and trained professionals.

## Future work

Planned directions and opportunities for contributors:
- Standardize an observation form (fields for size, contents, GPS, photo angles, substrate) and publish sample datasets.
- Build a community science portal for vetted contributions by citizen scientists and researchers.
- Explore automated image classification (non-invasive) for quick morphology tagging; ensure privacy and ethical use of sensitive location data.
- Partner with wildlife biologists to create best-practice guidelines for non-invasive genetic sampling and long-term monitoring (procedures to be documented by accredited labs).
- Educational outreach: create printable field guides, classroom modules, and workshops to teach safe, ethical sign interpretation.

Contributions are welcome — please open an issue or pull request. For data-sensitive contributions (e.g., den locations), coordinate via maintainers to protect wildlife.

---

## A final note

Studying scat is an honest, practical way to learn about wolves without needing to see them. We aim for clarity, rigor, and a touch of humor — because science and learning should be enjoyable while remaining respectful of the animals and environments we study.
